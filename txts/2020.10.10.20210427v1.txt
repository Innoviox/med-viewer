 9   Abstract:
10   The present work aims to give a contribution to the understanding of the highly infectious
11   pandemic caused by the COVID-19 in the African continent. The study focuses on the
12   modelling and the forecasting of COVID-19 spread in the most affected African continent,
13   namely: Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Egypt and South Africa and for the sake of comparison
14   two of the most affected European country are also considered, namely: France and Italy. To
15   this end, an epidemiological SEIQRDP model is presented, which is an adaptation of the classic
16   SIR model widely used in mathematical epidemiology. In order to better coincide with the
17   preventive measures taken by the governments to deal with the spread of COVID-19, this
18   model considers the quarantine. For the identification of the model‚Äôs parameters, official data
19   of the pandemic up to August 1st, 2020 are considered. The results show that the number of
20   infections due to the use of quarantine is expected to be very low provided the isolation is
21   effective. However, it is increasing in some countries with the early lifting of containment.
22   Finally, the information provided by the SEIQRDP model could help to establish a realistic
23   assessment of the short-term pandemic situation. Moreover, this will help maintain the most
24   appropriate and necessary public health measures after the lockdown lifting.
25
26   Keywords: COVID-19; SEIQRDP model; reproduction number; African continent.

28       1. Introduction
29   The year 2020 is and will be forever marked by the COVID-19 pandemic caused by the highly
30   infectious disease SARS-CoV-2. According to the World Health Organization, since its first
31   appearance in late 2019 in the Wuhan region of China, the Covid-19 has affected more than 6
32   million people and has caused the death of almost 400 thousand people worldwide, during the
33   first half of 2020. However, the spread of the disease and the damage caused by it did not
34   happen in the same way and at the same time for all continents. Indeed, taking the case of the
35   African continent, the COVID-19 pandemic reached a significant point by exceeding 180,000
36   cases in early June with more than 5,000 deaths. Although the pandemic has reached a
37   worrying threshold, the spread of the disease in Africa has not followed the exponential path as
38   in the rest of the world (e.g. Europe and United States). According to WHO‚Äôs analysis, the
39   relatively low mortality rate compared to other continents is likely due to the demographic
40   nature of the continent. Indeed, demographically, Africa is considered as the youngest
41   continent, with more than 60% of the population under the age of 25. However, the disease still
42   represents a danger for the African population, particularly, the South and African North. In
43   fact, according to the WHO, the above-mentioned regions account for more than 56% of the
44   cases recorded in Africa.
45   To date, in the absence of an effective vaccine or treatment against COVID-19, African
46   governments have taken several measures to limit the spread of the disease as much as possible
47   (e.g. quarantine, social distancing, lockdown, curfew, masks, etc.). Furthermore, during this
48   anti-COVID-19 battle, in addition to medical and biological research, theoretical studies based
49   on mathematical models can also play an important role in understanding the pandemic
50   dynamics (e.g. predicting the inflection point and of the end time). These models are therefore
51   an important decision-making tool for coping with this pandemic. Since the apparition of the
52   disease, the literature has witnessed an avalanche of work on modelling and predicting the
53   behaviour of the COVID-19 pandemic. However, most of these works have focused on China
54   [1-5], Europe [6-11] and the United States [7, 11-13], which is understandable because these
55   are the regions which were the first and most affected by COVID-19. Nevertheless, studies on
56   the African continent are practically negligible compared to those dedicated to the above-
57   mentioned regions [14-16].
58   The present paper aims to present a contribution on the modelling and the forecasting of
59   COVID-19 spread in the most affected African continent, namely: Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia,
60   Egypt and South Africa. In addition, for the sake of comparison two European countries are
61   considered, namely: France and Italy. Thereby, an epidemic SEIQRDP model is presented,
62   which is an adaptation of the classic SIR model widely used in mathematical epidemiology. In
63   the next section, we present the data and the model. Section 3 presents the results of the
64   application of the model for the above-mentioned countries through the estimation of the
65   epidemic parameters and the adjustment of the model. The discussion of the results obtained as
66   well as a forecast analysis, are presented in the same section. The last section provides some
67   future directions for using the SEIQRDP model to control and prevent the spread of the
68   COVID-19virus.

103        2.2. SEIQRDP Model
104   Using a pandemic modeling makes it possible to understand, describe and forecast its behavior
105   and its spread. Indeed, the mathematical models assess hypotheses, indicate trends, and help
106   develop public health responses by estimating risks, in real time, during an epidemic.
107   Nevertheless, modeling is a simplified representation of reality. Its precision is limited here by
108   the ignorance of certain factors and mechanism of propagation of COVID-19.
109   In this work, the SEIQRDP epidemiological model with seven different components, proposed
110   by [2] is used. The model is an adaptation of the classic SEIR model [18, 19], widely used to
111   study the COVID-19 pandemic in many countries with variations in components and
112   parameters for adaptations to regions and study period [16, 19-23]. The present model
113   considers the effect of quarantine adopted by many countries as an effective means of
114   preventing the spread. The stochastic SEIQRDP model is specified by the following system of

155   However, one of the most discussed parameters in the current COVID-19 pandemic is the basic
156   reproductive number [2, 24-26]. Biologically, this number, represented by R0 , corresponds to
157   the number of new infections caused by an infected individual in a susceptible population. One
158   of the main reasons of the importance of the basic reproduction number is that it determines the
159   fraction of the population that needs to be immunized for the epidemic to start to decline. The
160   reproductive number will vary during an epidemic for two reasons. On the one hand, the public
161   health measures are put in place, like the lockdowns, social distancing, and other mitigation
162   strategies to keep the case count and death toll low in this pandemic. On the other hand, the
163   epidemic spreads and people who have recovered are protected against reinfection (at least in
164   the near future). Thus, the population becomes immune.
165   If the R0 is above 1, each infection breeds more, and the outbreak will continue to grow. When
166   it falls below 1, the outbreak will continue but at a lower death rate.

388      4. Conclusion
389   In this paper, mathematical model for the coronavirus COVID-19 epidemic development is
390   performed for the most affected African countries based on an epidemiological SEIQRDP
391   model, which is an adaptation of the classic SIR model widely used in mathematical
392   epidemiology. The SEIQRDP model takes quarantine into account to match with the medical
393   security measures adapted by many countries as an effective way of preventing the spread of
394   the coronavirus COVID-19. Furthermore, considering all major parameters of the progression
395   of the coronavirus, several analytic results established, while proving all the necessary
396   properties for epidemiological relevance. This model allowed us to estimate the main epidemic
397   parameters and forecast the evolution of the reproduction number of the disease over time in
398   order to assess the epidemic situation and the effect of the applied preventive health measures.
399   An acceptable agreement between the statistical data and the curves of the model was
400   established. This indicates the suitability of the SEIQRDP in modelling the spread of the
401   COVID-19 disease for the considered countries.
402   On the other hand, according to the statistical, fitted and predicted curves, we noticed that the
403   epidemic peaks are between mid-April and mid-August, after which the epidemic declines.
404   This situation can be explained by the policy adopted by African countries, aimed at effectively
405   combating this virus. In addition, this confirms the effectiveness of the lockdown measures and
406   curfews taken early enough to slow the spread of the disease. Most countries have implemented
407   these measures as soon as the first case is detected. In France, it took 52 days after the first case
408   to take such measures. In addition to this factor, there are many other factors that contribute to
409   the robust and rapid response of these countries. The other main explanation is the youth of the
410   African population, around 60% of the population under 25 years of age. In addition, the
411   population density is lower in Africa, thus limiting the spread of the virus, as is the low
412   mobility of African populations in comparison with Western populations, where most of the
413   cases remain concentrated in the capitals and large cities.
414   However, caution should probably follow. A general lockdown lifting seems unlikely, while
415   the gradual lifting of lockdown experienced by the countries under study by city, depending on
416   the mortality rate and the number of new infections observed, may be reasonable in compliance
417   with the measures of security. However, Algeria and Tunisia may experience new peaks
418   expected mid-October, due to the lifting of lockdown, where countries have allowed the
419   reopening of cafes, restaurants and gymnasiums as well as the resumption of tourism domestic
420   and interurban. Nevertheless, the three Maghreb countries will have to strengthen the control of
421   health measures, to avoid the epidemic resumption. The crucial element will remain the
422   generalization of tests which will remain one of the main ingredients in the fight against
423   epidemics in the country. All these precautions will effectively reduce the number of basic
