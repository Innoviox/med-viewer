 1    Abstract
 2    Alzheimer disease(AD) affects profoundly the quality of human life. Quantifying the
 3    severity degree of Alzheimer dementia for an individual person is critical for the early
 4    diagnose and prescription for delaying the further dementia progression. However, the
 5    quantitative diagnose for human subjects of the mild cognitively impairment(MCI) or
 6    AD with the different degree of dementia severity is a difficult task due to both the very
 7    broad distribution of dementia severities and the lack of good quantitative determinant
 8    to assess it. Here, based on cortical thickness profiles of human brains measured by
 9    magnetic resonance experiment, a new algebraic approach is presented for the
10    personalized quantification of the severity degree of AD, ranging from 0 for the basin
11    of cognitively normal state to 1 for AD state. Now one can unfold the broad distribution
12    of dementia severities and corresponding cortex regions of human brains with MCI or
13    AD by the different severity degree.

 1    Introduction
 2    Alzheimerâ€™s disease (AD) is one of the most well-known neurodegenerative diseases,
 3    and it profoundly affects the quality of human life. Various causes of AD are known,
 4    and clinical treatments for preventing or delaying the progression of AD are currently
 5    practiced. The precise diagnosis of AD requires not only the systematic identification
 6    of cohorts that classify the different stages of brain progression toward AD but also the
 7    estimation of the severity degree of AD for a given individual (Galvin et al., 2012,
 8    Solomon and Soininen, 2015, Raj et al., 2012). The symptoms of AD appear in various
 9    forms in the human body, behavior, and cognition, yet the direct anatomical evidences
10    appear in the structural change within the brain cortex (Hojjati et al., 2019, Kim et al.,
11    2020, Qiu et al., 2020, Tetreault et al., 2020). Among these evidences is the
12    degradation of the cortical thickness of the human brain, which is one of the imprints
13    of AD. Such physical change can be monitored through the neuro-image analysis, for
14    example the magnetic resonance image (MRI) analysis of the brain (Hartikainen et al.,
15    2012, Im et al., 2008, Kim et al., 2014, Lebedev et al., 2013, Paternico et al., 2016,
16    Querbes et al., 2009).
17    The anatomical degradation of the cortical thickness becomes more pronounced as
18    the degree of dementia becomes greater (Im et al., 2008, Querbes et al., 2009).
19    Therefore, there is a rationale for taking advantage of the degree of cortical
20    degradation for determining the dementia cohort and estimating the severity of
21    dementia progression. Given the big data information of cortical thickness for a single
22    subject, however, we are faced with several obstacles to overcome in accomplishing
23    abovementioned tasks. First, we noted that the person-to-person fluctuations in
24    cortical thickness of an individual may overwhelm the degradation in cortical thickness.
25    In clinical cases, we frequently observed that the average cortical thickness of some

 1    cognitively normal people is thinner than that of people with AD, which appears to
 2    contrast conventional view. The second obstacle is the ambiguity in what we should
 3    do if two different cohorts have a difference in the cortical thickness in brain regions
 4    that have little to do with AD. In principle, we should construct some determinants for
 5    distinguishing a cognitively normal person from a person with AD based on cortical
 6    thickness, but in practice, we are confronted with the differences in cortical thickness
 7    in regions of the cortex that are unrelated to the pathogenesis of AD. We noted that
 8    the abundant existence of such unrelated regions is an intrinsic source that increases
 9    the uncertainty of the dementia determinants and hinders the appropriate construction
10    of good classifier and predictor for AD.
11    In this study, instead of dealing with all 327,684 vertices point on the whole cortex of
12    a human brain, we demonstrated that the consideration of a few hundred essential
13    vertices were enough for distinguishing CN, MCI, AD cohorts each other. With cortical
14    thickness data at these essential vertices, we defined the statistical score matrix and
15    the covariance correlation matrix between human subjects as a set of classifier and
16    predictor for AD. Unlike the conventional view that the degradation of the cortical
17    thickness of human brain was responsible for AD, the singular valued decomposition
18    analysis of the statistical score matrix developed in this study implicated that the
19    simultaneous consideration of both thinner and thicker cortical regions together
20    compared to those of CN are important and necessary for the precise diagnose of the
21    severity of AD.

 2    Identification of essential ROI vertices at which the distributions of
 3    cortical thickness of CN, MCI, AD subjects are distinguishable

 5    thinner than that of CN or MCI subjects, the distribution curves of averaged cortical

 1    Discussion
 2    Based on the cortical thickness data of 1516 human brain images from the ADNI data
 3    set, we presented a new algebraic approach for both (1) the identification of the cohort
 4    (CN, MCI, AD) a given subject would belong to and (2) the quantitative estimation of
 5    the severity degree of AD for a given new person (Figure 4). A total of 1516 human
 6    brain MR images were partitioned into four groups by the average cortical thickness
 7    of each subject. Out of 327,684 vertices on the cortex, a few hundred essential ROI
 8    vertices for each group were identified, which were enough to distinguish the cortical
 9    thickness distribution of the CN, MCI, and AD cohorts from each other. Statistical score
10    matrices using the cortical thickness on the essential ROI vertices were constructed
11    as a classifier for determining the cohort of a given subject. Out of 547 CN, 722 MCI,
12    and 247 AD subjects predetermined clinically, the success ratio for recognizing their
13    cohort was 92% with CN, 69% with MCI, and 91% with AD subjects. On the other hand,
14    the stratified 3-fold cross-validation test gave the correct prediction rate of 84% with
15    CN, 59% with MCI, and 82% in subjects with AD; this is in agreement with the results
16    of clinical determination. Using the quantitative severity degree of AD for subjects, we
17    could explain the reason why the inevitable uncertainty in the determination of the MCI
18    cohort arouse by the very broad distribution of the severity degree of dementia which
19    MCI subjects possess intrinsically. We suggested that the severity degree of AD
20    presented in this study would be a realistic measure for the quantitative personalized
21    diagnosis of a given subject instead of tri-partitioning the classification of a subjectâ€™s
22    cohort only by CN, MCI or AD. It is the continuous degree for a given subject along
23    the scale from 0 for the basin of CN state to 1 for the basin of AD state. One could sort
24    out quantitatively the broad spectrum of the severity degree of dementia for MCI or AD
25    subjects with the different severity degree of dementia in that whether they are prone
